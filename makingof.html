TITLE: TOO LATE TO NOTICE

CHARACTERS:

THE MAN (30s):
Dressed in a corporate coat. He is handsome but looks drained. He exists in a self-imposed bubble of isolation.
THE STRANGER (40s-50s):
Homeless. Wears layers. He is not a villain; he is a watcher.

ACT 1: THE ROUTINE

EXT. SUBWAY ENTRANCE (STAIRS) - NIGHT
(The Anchor - Routine)
[VISUAL] The Man stands at the top of the concrete stairs. It is a standard, cold city night. Blue-tinted lighting.
He buttons his coat to his chin. He puts on large noise-canceling headphones. He stares at his phone screen.
He descends the stairs like a zombie. People brush past him, but he doesn't flinch. He is in a bubble. He is isolated from the sound of the city.
THE MAN (V.O.)
Do you believe in miracles? Or do you believe in cycles?
INT. SUBWAY PLATFORM - NIGHT
(The Foreshadowing)
[VISUAL] The Man stands near the edge of the platform, waiting for the train. He is framed in the foreground, sharp focus, scrolling on his phone.
[BACKGROUND ACTION] Deep in the background, sitting on a metal bench, is THE STRANGER.
He is slightly out of focus (bokeh). While other commuters rush by in a blur, The Stranger is perfectly still. He is looking directly at The Man.
Note: The audience might miss him on the first watch, but he is there.
THE MAN (V.O.)
We spend our lives waiting. Waiting for the weekend. Waiting for the train. Waiting to feel something different.
INT. SUBWAY CAR / OFFICE / APARTMENT - NIGHT
(The Routine Montage)
[VISUAL] FAST CUTS. The editing accelerates to show the suffocating repetition.

  1. SUBWAY CAR: The Man sits. Reflection in the dark window.
  2. OFFICE: Typing at a desk in a dark room.
  3. APARTMENT: Entering. Throwing keys. Opening the fridge. Closing the fridge.
  4. REPEAT: Walking down the stairs (Day 2).
  5. REPEAT: Scrolling on the train (Day 3). (The Stranger is visible in the background again, sitting two rows back, watching).

THE MAN (V.O.)
My life is a straight line. Point A to Point B. No detours. No surprises. Just the safety of the script.

ACT 2: THE CHANGE

EXT. SUBWAY ENTRANCE (STAIRS) - NIGHT
(The Anchor - The Incident)
[VISUAL] Same framing as Act 1. The Man approaches the stairs. Headphones on. Phone out.
He takes the first step down.
ACTION: THE STRANGER (the man from the background) lunges from the shadows. He snatches the phone from The Man’s hand.
THE MAN
Hey! Stop!
The Stranger sprints back up the stairs. The Man reacts—shock, then adrenaline. He scrambles up and chases.
EXT. CITY STREETS - CONTINUOUS
(The Chase)
[VISUAL - STEP PRINTING]
The image blurs and streaks (Wong Kar-wai style). The Man is sprinting, coat flapping. The city lights smear into lines.
He is breathing heavy. Panic. Chaos.
He chases The Stranger through the streets, dodging pedestrians, but the gap widens.
He turns a corner onto a busy shopping street.
He pushes through the throng of people, searching desperately, but he is eventually swallowed by the crowd.
He stops. He spins around.
The Stranger is gone. Just a sea of anonymous backs.
THE MAN (V.O.)
And just like that... the screen went black.
EXT. PARK BENCH - LATER THAT NIGHT
(The Confrontation)
[VISUAL] A quiet, dark park.
The Man walks past a bench. He spots THE STRANGER sitting there, smoking a cigarette. The smoke swirls in the cold air. The phone sits on the bench next to him, untouched.
The Man approaches, out of breath, defeated.
THE MAN
Why... why did you make me run?
THE STRANGER
(Calmly, exhaling smoke)
Because you looked like you were sleepwalking. I've been watching you.
THE MAN
Watching me?
THE STRANGER
Every day. At the station. You stand in the same spot. You look at the same screen. You never look up.
The Man sits on the other end of the bench. He picks up his phone.
THE STRANGER
I used to be you. The suit. The schedule. The tunnel vision. I walked past this bench for ten years and never saw it.
(He leans in)
Don't wait until you lose everything to realize you were part of something bigger. Don't be too late to notice.
The Stranger stands up, dropping the cigarette and crushing it under his boot. He gestures toward the city center, where a warm, golden glow is rising.
THE STRANGER
Go. Take a walk. Really look at them.
He walks away into the shadows.

ACT 3: THE CHANGE (SONDER)

EXT. CHRISTMAS MARKET - MOMENTS LATER
(The Awakening)
[VISUAL] Drastic Color Shift.
The Man walks from the dark park into the Düsseldorf Christmas Market.
The screen is filled with GOLD, RED, and GREEN.
Fairy lights. Steam from Glühwein. Crowds.
The Man slows down. He looks around, stunned.
[VISUAL VFX - BLOB TRACKING / POV]
We switch to THE MAN'S POV. The camera tracks specific people. As it locks onto them, a simple, elegant text box tracks above their heads.

  1. TARGET: A MAN holding a teddy bear. TEXT: "Buying this for his daughter. He hasn't seen her in a year."
  2. TARGET: A WOMAN laughing loudly. TEXT: "Just beat cancer. Celebrating being alive."
  3. TARGET: AN ELDERLY COUPLE sharing a crepe. TEXT: "Their 50th Christmas together."

[VISUAL] Cut back to The Man. He watches them. He isn't annoyed by the crowd anymore; he feels connected. A small smile forms.
THE MAN (V.O.)
Suddenly, the noise wasn't just noise. It was a symphony. Every stranger... a story.
EXT. SUBWAY ENTRANCE (STAIRS) - THE NEXT MORNING
(The New Routine)
[VISUAL] Bright Morning Light. The sun is breaking through the winter gray.
The Man stands at the exact same spot at the top of the stairs.
He is wearing his headphones. He is holding his phone. It looks like the loop is about to repeat.
ACTION: He pauses. He looks at the phone screen.
Then, slowly, deliberately:

  1. He takes the headphones off his head.
  2. He slides the phone into his deep coat pocket.

He takes a deep breath of the crisp morning air. He looks around at the city, taking it all in.
He steps forward and begins to walk down the stairs, head up, watching the people around him.
THE MAN (V.O.)
Life is short. But it is wide. You just have to be brave enough to press pause.
FADE TO BLACK.
TITLE CARD:
TOO LATE TO NOTICE

The idea

The core idea of our short film, “Too Late To Notice” revolves around the concept of "sonder" - the profound realization that every random passerby has a life as vivid and complex as your own. The story follows a character who is stuck in a monotonous routine, but a fleeting moment with a stranger completely transforms his perspective. We wanted to step away from an egotistical worldview and encourage the audience to reflect on their deeper connection to others. Visually and emotionally, our main inspiration came from 90s Hong Kong cinema, particularly the highly atmospheric films of Wong Kar-wai.

Concept

To bring this vision to life, we aimed for a nostalgic, slightly "low-budget," and old-timey aesthetic. We really wanted to use "step printing", which was that classic Wong Kar-wai look. It was the perfect way to visually show "sonder.". To get on the same page and organize our thoughts, we built a mind map on our Miro board. This helped us brainstorm and connect our three main pillars: the specific Wong Kar-wai visual style, the thematic depth of "sonder," and how our filming locations could tie those elements together.

Film process

We shot primarily in Köln and Düsseldorf. After some location scouting, we chose a subway station in Cologne because it naturally possessed the classic, gritty aesthetic we needed for that specific Wong Kar-wai vibe. As a two-person crew handling the directing, filming, and acting, our biggest challenge was nailing the composition of each shot and translating our storyboard into reality. However, having a solid script and storyboard gave us a great safety net, which allowed us to experiment on set. We embraced a spontaneous, natural approach, giving ourselves the freedom to deviate from the storyboard to capture more genuine moments. That experimentation was definitely the most fun part of the shoot.

Post-processing

Because we were such a small crew, we needed a highly logical and collaborative editing workflow. We set up a shared project in DaVinci Resolve so we could both work on the film simultaneously. We actually started by having each of us edit our own version of the film first. This was an incredible learning experience, as we got to see each other's pacing and stylistic choices. Ultimately, we merged the best elements of both edits, combining our directorial visions into a final piece that truly captures the essence of the "sonder" theme.
